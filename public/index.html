About

Marissa Gates

Instructional designer specializing in compliance simulations, technical training, and educational technology. I turn complex operational knowledge into learning experiences that change behavior.

Background

My path to instructional design isn't typical ‚Äî and that's exactly why it works. I spent years in systems management and fire protection operations, where I saw firsthand how training either prepared people for critical decisions or failed them at the worst possible moment.

I became obsessed with the gap between knowing the rules and executing under pressure. That obsession led me to instructional design, where I could actually build the experiences that close that gap.

What I Bring

I combine deep technical domain knowledge with evidence-based learning design. I don't just translate SME content into eLearning ‚Äî I analyze the performance gap, identify the decisions that matter, and build interactions that force realistic practice with meaningful consequences.

I'm also a developer. I build Progressive Web Apps, automate workflows, and integrate AI tools into the design process ‚Äî not as a gimmick, but as a genuine force multiplier that lets me spend more time on design decisions and less on production work.

Design Philosophy

Every project starts with one question: what does the learner need to DO differently? Not know ‚Äî do. That question drives everything: the content that makes the cut, the interactions I build, and the feedback the learner receives.

I use action mapping (Cathy Moore), SAM for iterative development, and gamification frameworks tailored to the content type. I believe in no-nerfing ‚Äî wrong answers should carry weight. And I believe the best training feels less like a course and more like a rehearsal for real life.

The IROL Field Call

A compliance training simulation that replaces lecture-based instruction with decision-driven practice. Built for fire suppression technicians navigating new electronic reporting requirements.

The Challenge

Western Fire Prevention faced a critical training gap when Nevada adopted the new IROL electronic reporting system. Under IFC 110.3.1, every licensed service contractor must electronically report fire protection impairments and deficiencies ‚Äî with strict timelines, mandatory fields, and financial penalties.

The existing training was a 32-page PowerPoint delivered in a single session. Five complex topic areas compressed into one lecture with no opportunity to practice decision-making under realistic conditions.

The core performance gap wasn't knowledge ‚Äî it was execution under pressure. Technicians understood the rules in theory but made critical errors when juggling time constraints, customer relationships, and multi-stop daily schedules.

The Approach

I used action mapping to work backward from the business goal: technicians report accurately under IROL within required timelines. Every simulation decision maps to a real field error.

The content detox was aggressive. From 32 pages of lecture material, I extracted 6 critical decisions and built the entire simulation around practicing those decisions with realistic consequences. Reference content was separated into standalone Rise 360 modules ‚Äî accessible when needed but not required for the simulation.

The dual-variable architecture tracks both Compliance (legal accuracy) and Client Trust (relationship management) simultaneously. This creates authentic dilemmas: a technician who maximizes compliance by leading with legal threats creates customer panic. One who maximizes trust by downplaying findings creates legal exposure. The optimal path requires balancing both.

Key Design Decisions

The delayed consequence system is the simulation's most distinctive feature. If a learner downplays an impairment during the client conversation, trust initially goes up. But two weeks later in the simulation timeline, the fire department contacts the customer about the report ‚Äî and trust collapses. This teaches something a quiz cannot: short-term comfort and long-term trust are not the same thing.

The four conditional endings (Strategic Stewardship, By the Book, The People Pleaser, The Wake-Up Call) create replayability and allow the simulation to function as both training and assessment without revealing "correct answers."

AI-Enhanced Workflow

I integrated AI tools at multiple stages ‚Äî scenario scripting, variable logic design, content analysis, quiz bank generation, and visual asset creation. Every AI output went through SME review. AI accelerated the production timeline by approximately 40%, freeing time for design decisions and SME collaboration.

Results

The 5-module course architecture (one Storyline simulation + four Rise 360 reference modules) includes an adaptive pathway: new hires complete knowledge modules first, experienced technicians start with the simulation and are routed to specific modules based on performance gaps.

Designed evaluation metrics include 95% accuracy on compliance classification, first-attempt IROL acceptance, and zero missed reporting deadlines in the post-training period.
